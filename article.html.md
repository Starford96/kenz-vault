grid w-full grid-cols-12"><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 col-span-12 max-w-none [&amp;:not(:first-child)]:mt-sm"><p class="mb-sm last:mb-0"><span>Over the past five months, our team has been running an experiment: building and shipping an internal beta of a software product with</span><b><span> 0 lines of manually-written code</span></b><span>.</span></p></div><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 col-span-12 max-w-none [&amp;:not(:first-child)]:mt-sm"><p class="mb-sm last:mb-0"><span>The product has internal daily users and external alpha testers. It ships, deploys, breaks, and gets fixed. What’s different is that every line of code—application logic, tests, CI configuration, documentation, observability, and internal tooling—has been written by Codex. We estimate that we built this in about 1/10th the time it would have taken to write the code by hand.</span></p></div><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 col-span-12 max-w-none [&amp;:not(:first-child)]:mt-sm"><p class="mb-sm last:mb-0"><b><span>Humans steer. Agents execute.</span></b></p></div><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 col-span-12 max-w-none [&amp;:not(:first-child)]:mt-sm"><p class="mb-sm last:mb-0"><span>We intentionally chose this constraint so we would build what was necessary to increase engineering velocity by orders of magnitude. We had weeks to ship what ended up being a million lines of code. To do that, we needed to understand what changes when a software engineering team’s primary job is no longer to write code, but to design environments, specify intent, and build feedback loops that allow Codex agents to do reliable work.</span></p></div><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 col-span-12 max-w-none [&amp;:not(:first-child)]:mt-sm"><p class="mb-sm last:mb-0"><span>This post is about what we learned by building a brand new product with a team of agents—what broke, what compounded, and how to maximize our one truly scarce resource: human time and attention.</span></p></div><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 col-span-12 max-w-none [&amp;:not(:first-child)]:mt-xl"><div class="@container max-w-container grid w-full grid-cols-12 toc-content-heading scroll-mt-[calc(var(--header-h)+var(--toc-button-h))]" id="we-started-with-an-empty-git-repository"><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 full-grid-content:@md:col-span-12 full-grid-content:@md:col-start-1 col-span-12 max-w-none"><h2 class="text-h3 scroll-mt-[calc(var(--header-h)+var(--toc-button-h))]"><span>We started with an empty git repository</span></h2></div></div></div><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 col-span-12 max-w-none [&amp;:not(:first-child)]:mt-sm"><p class="mb-sm last:mb-0"><span>The first commit to an empty repository landed in late August 2025.</span></p></div><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 col-span-12 max-w-none [&amp;:not(:first-child)]:mt-sm"><p class="mb-sm last:mb-0"><span>The initial scaffold—repository structure, CI configuration, formatting rules, package manager setup, and application framework—was generated by Codex CLI using GPT‑5, guided by a small set of existing templates. Even the initial AGENTS.md file that directs agents how to work in the repository was itself written by Codex.</span></p></div><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 col-span-12 max-w-none [&amp;:not(:first-child)]:mt-sm"><p class="mb-sm last:mb-0"><span>There was no pre-existing human-written code to anchor the system. From the beginning, the repository was shaped by the agent.</span></p></div><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 col-span-12 max-w-none [&amp;:not(:first-child)]:mt-sm"><p class="mb-sm last:mb-0"><span>Five months later, the repository contains on the order of a million lines of code across application logic, infrastructure, tooling, documentation, and internal developer utilities. Over that period, roughly 1,500 pull requests have been opened and merged with a small team of just three engineers driving Codex. This translates to an average throughput of 3.5 PRs per engineer per day, and surprisingly the throughput has </span><i><span>increased</span></i><span> as the team has grown to now seven engineers. Importantly, this wasn’t output for output’s sake: the product has been used by hundreds of users internally, including daily internal power users.</span></p></div><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 col-span-12 max-w-none [&amp;:not(:first-child)]:mt-sm"><p class="mb-sm last:mb-0"><span>Throughout the development process, humans never directly contributed any code. This became a core philosophy for the team: </span><b><span>no manually-written code</span></b><span>.</span></p></div><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 col-span-12 max-w-none [&amp;:not(:first-child)]:mt-xl"><div class="@container max-w-container grid w-full grid-cols-12 toc-content-heading scroll-mt-[calc(var(--header-h)+var(--toc-button-h))]" id="redefining-the-role-of-the-engineer"><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 full-grid-content:@md:col-span-12 full-grid-content:@md:col-start-1 col-span-12 max-w-none"><h2 class="text-h3 scroll-mt-[calc(var(--header-h)+var(--toc-button-h))]"><span>Redefining the role of the engineer</span></h2></div></div></div><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 col-span-12 max-w-none [&amp;:not(:first-child)]:mt-sm"><p class="mb-sm last:mb-0"><span>The lack of hands-on human coding </span><b><span>introduced a different kind of engineering work, focused on systems, scaffolding, and leverage</span></b><span>.</span></p></div><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 col-span-12 max-w-none [&amp;:not(:first-child)]:mt-sm"><p class="mb-sm last:mb-0"><span>Early progress was slower than we expected, not because Codex was incapable, but because the environment was underspecified. The agent lacked the tools, abstractions, and internal structure required to make progress toward high-level goals. The primary job of our engineering team became enabling the agents to do useful work.</span></p></div><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 col-span-12 max-w-none [&amp;:not(:first-child)]:mt-sm"><p class="mb-sm last:mb-0"><span>In practice, this meant working depth-first: breaking down larger goals into smaller building blocks (design, code, review, test, etc), prompting the agent to construct those blocks, and using them to unlock more complex tasks. When something failed, the fix was almost never “try harder.” Because the only way to make progress was to get Codex to do the work, human engineers always stepped into the task and asked: “what capability is missing, and how do we make it both legible and enforceable for the agent?”</span></p></div><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 col-span-12 max-w-none [&amp;:not(:first-child)]:mt-sm"><p class="mb-sm last:mb-0"><span>Humans interact with the system almost entirely through prompts: an engineer describes a task, runs the agent, and allows it to open a pull request. To drive a PR to completion, we instruct Codex to review its own changes locally, request additional specific agent reviews both locally and in the cloud, respond to any human or agent given feedback, and iterate in a loop until all agent reviewers are satisfied (effectively this is a </span><a href="https://ghuntley.com/loop/" class="transition ease-curve-a duration-250 text-primary-100 hover:text-primary-60 relative underline-offset-[0.25rem] decoration-1 underline" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer"><u class="decoration-1 underline-offset-4"><span>Ralph Wiggum Loop</span></u>⁠<span class="sr-only">(opens in a new window)</span></a><span>). Codex uses our standard development tools directly (gh, local scripts, and repository-embedded skills) to gather context without humans copying and pasting into the CLI.</span></p></div><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 col-span-12 max-w-none [&amp;:not(:first-child)]:mt-sm"><p class="mb-sm last:mb-0"><span>Humans may review pull requests, but aren’t required to. Over time, we’ve pushed almost all review effort towards being handled agent-to-agent.</span></p></div><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 col-span-12 max-w-none [&amp;:not(:first-child)]:mt-xl"><div class="@container max-w-container grid w-full grid-cols-12 toc-content-heading scroll-mt-[calc(var(--header-h)+var(--toc-button-h))]" id="increasing-application-legibility"><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 full-grid-content:@md:col-span-12 full-grid-content:@md:col-start-1 col-span-12 max-w-none"><h2 class="text-h3 scroll-mt-[calc(var(--header-h)+var(--toc-button-h))]"><span>Increasing application legibility</span></h2></div></div></div><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 col-span-12 max-w-none [&amp;:not(:first-child)]:mt-sm"><p class="mb-sm last:mb-0"><span>As code throughput increased, our bottleneck became human QA capacity. Because the fixed constraint has been human time and attention, we’ve worked to add more capabilities to the agent by making things like the application UI, logs, and app metrics themselves directly legible to Codex.</span></p></div><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 col-span-12 max-w-none [&amp;:not(:first-child)]:mt-sm"><p class="mb-sm last:mb-0"><span>For example, we made the app bootable per git worktree, so Codex could launch and drive one instance per change. We also wired the Chrome DevTools Protocol into the agent runtime and created skills for working with DOM snapshots, screenshots, and navigation. This enabled Codex to reproduce bugs, validate fixes, and reason about UI behavior directly.</span></p></div><div class="col-span-12 group [&amp;:not(:first-child)]:mt-xl When documentation falls short, we promote the rule into code</span></p></div><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 col-span-12 max-w-none [&amp;:not(:first-child)]:mt-xl"><div class="@container max-w-container grid w-full grid-cols-12 toc-content-heading scroll-mt-[calc(var(--header-h)+var(--toc-button-h))]" id="throughput-changes-the-merge-philosophy"><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 full-grid-content:@md:col-span-12 full-grid-content:@md:col-start-1 col-span-12 max-w-none"><h2 class="text-h3 scroll-mt-[calc(var(--header-h)+var(--toc-button-h))]"><span>Throughput changes the merge philosophy</span></h2></div></div></div><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 col-span-12 max-w-none [&amp;:not(:first-child)]:mt-sm"><p class="mb-sm last:mb-0"><span>As Codex’s throughput increased, many conventional engineering norms became counterproductive.</span></p></div><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 col-span-12 max-w-none [&amp;:not(:first-child)]:mt-sm"><p class="mb-sm last:mb-0"><span>The repository operates with minimal blocking merge gates. Pull requests are short-lived. Test flakes are often addressed with follow-up runs rather than blocking progress indefinitely. In a system where agent throughput far exceeds human attention, corrections are cheap, and waiting is expensive.</span></p></div><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 col-span-12 max-w-none [&amp;:not(:first-child)]:mt-sm"><p class="mb-sm last:mb-0"><span>This would be irresponsible in a low-throughput environment. Here, it’s often the right tradeoff.</span></p></div><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 col-span-12 max-w-none [&amp;:not(:first-child)]:mt-xl"><div class="@container max-w-container grid full-grid-content:@md:col-span-12 full-grid-content:@md:col-start-1 col-span-12 max-w-none"><h2 class="text-h3 scroll-mt-[calc(var(--header-h)+var(--toc-button-h))]"><span>What “agent-generated” actually means</span></h2></div></div></div><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 col-span-12 max-w-none [&amp;:not(:first-child)]:mt-sm"><p class="mb-sm last:mb-0"><span>When we say the codebase is generated by Codex agents, we mean everything in the codebase.</span></p></div><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 col-span-12 max-w-none [&amp;:not(:first-child)]:mt-sm"><p class="mb-sm last:mb-0"><span>Agents produce:</span></p></div><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 col-span-12 max-w-none [&amp;:not(:first-child)]:mt-sm"><ul class="mb-md marker:text-inherit last:mb-0 [:where(ul,ol)_&amp;]:mt-2 list-disc [:where(ul,ol)_&amp;]:list-[circle] ps-2xs mx-3xs"><li class="mb-4xs last:mb-0"><span>Product code and tests</span></li><li class="mb-4xs last:mb-0"><span>CI configuration and release tooling</span></li><li class="mb-4xs last:mb-0"><span>Internal developer tools</span></li><li class="mb-4xs last:mb-0"><span>Documentation and design history</span></li><li class="mb-4xs last:mb-0"><span>Evaluation harnesses</span></li><li class="mb-4xs last:mb-0"><span>Review comments and responses</span></li><li class="mb-4xs last:mb-0"><span>Scripts that manage the repository itself</span></li><li class="mb-4xs last:mb-0"><span>Production dashboard definition files</span></li></ul></div><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 col-span-12 max-w-none [&amp;:not(:first-child)]:mt-sm"><p class="mb-sm last:mb-0"><span>Humans always remain in the loop, but work at a different layer of abstraction than we used to. We prioritize work, translate user feedback into acceptance criteria, and validate outcomes. When the agent struggles, we treat it as a signal: identify what is missing—tools, guardrails, documentation—and feed it back into the repository, always by having Codex itself write the fix.</span></p></div><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 col-span-12 max-w-none [&amp;:not(:first-child)]:mt-sm"><p class="mb-sm last:mb-0"><span>Agents use our standard development tools directly. They pull review feedback, respond inline, push updates, and often squash and merge their own pull requests.</span></p></div><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 col-span-12 max-w-none [&amp;:not(:first-child)]:mt-xl"><div class="@container max-w-container grid w-full grid-cols-12 toc-content-heading scroll-mt-[calc(var(--header-h)+var(--toc-button-h))]" id="increasing-levels-of-autonomy"><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 full-grid-content:@md:col-span-12 full-grid-content:@md:col-start-1 col-span-12 max-w-none"><h2 class="text-h3 scroll-mt-[calc(var(--header-h)+var(--toc-button-h))]"><span>Increasing levels of autonomy</span></h2></div></div></div><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 col-span-12 max-w-none [&amp;:not(:first-child)]:mt-sm"><p class="mb-sm last:mb-0"><span>As more of the development loop was encoded directly into the system—testing, validation, review, feedback handling, and recovery—the repository recently crossed a meaningful threshold where Codex can end-to-end drive a new feature.</span></p></div><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 col-span-12 max-w-none [&amp;:not(:first-child)]:mt-sm"><p class="mb-sm last:mb-0"><span>Given a single prompt, the agent can now:</span></p></div><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 col-span-12 max-w-none [&amp;:not(:first-child)]:mt-sm"><ul class="mb-md marker:text-inherit last:mb-0 [:where(ul,ol)_&amp;]:mt-2 list-disc [:where(ul,ol)_&amp;]:list-[circle] ps-2xs mx-3xs"><li class="mb-4xs last:mb-0"><span>Validate the current state of the codebase</span></li><li class="mb-4xs last:mb-0"><span>Reproduce a reported bug</span></li><li class="mb-4xs last:mb-0"><span>Record a video [&amp;:not(:first-child)]:mt-sm"><p class="mb-sm last:mb-0"><span>This behavior depends heavily on the specific structure and tooling of this repository and should not be assumed to generalize without similar investment—at least, not yet.</span></p></div><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 col-span-12 max-w-none [&amp;:not(:first-child)]:mt-xl"><div class="@container max-w-container grid full-grid-content:@md:col-span-12 full-grid-content:@md:col-start-1 col-span-12 max-w-none"><h2 class="text-h3 scroll-mt-[calc(var(--header-h)+var(--toc-button-h))]"><span>Entropy and garbage collection</span></h2></div></div></div><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 col-span-12 max-w-none [&amp;:not(:first-child)]:mt-sm"><p class="mb-sm last:mb-0"><b><span>Full agent autonomy also introduces novel problems. </span></b><span>Codex replicates patterns that already exist in the repository—even uneven or suboptimal ones. Over time, this inevitably leads to drift.</span></p></div><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 col-span-12 max-w-none [&amp;:not(:first-child)]:mt-sm"><p class="mb-sm last:mb-0"><span>Initially, humans addressed this manually. Our team used to spend every Friday (20% of the week) cleaning up “AI slop.” Unsurprisingly, that didn’t scale.</span></p></div><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 col-span-12 max-w-none [&amp;:not(:first-child)]:mt-sm"><p class="mb-sm last:mb-0"><span>Instead, we started encoding what we call “golden principles” directly into the repository and built a recurring cleanup process. These principles are opinionated, mechanical rules that keep the codebase legible and consistent for future agent runs. For example: (1) we prefer shared utility packages over hand-rolled helpers to keep invariants centralized, and (2) we don’t probe data “YOLO-style”—we validate boundaries or rely on typed SDKs so the agent can’t accidentally build on guessed shapes. On a regular cadence, we have a set of background Codex tasks that scan for deviations, update quality grades, and open targeted refactoring pull requests. Most of these can be reviewed in under a minute and automerged.</span></p></div><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 col-span-12 max-w-none [&amp;:not(:first-child)]:mt-sm"><p class="mb-sm last:mb-0"><span>This functions like garbage collection. Technical debt is like a high-interest loan: it’s almost always better to pay it down continuously in small increments than to let it compound and tackle it in painful bursts. Human taste is captured once, then enforced continuously on every line of code. This also lets us catch and resolve bad patterns on a daily basis, rather than letting them spread in the code base for days or weeks.</span></p></div><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 col-span-12 max-w-none [&amp;:not(:first-child)]:mt-xl"><div class="@container max-w-container grid w-full grid-cols-12 toc-content-heading scroll-mt-[calc(var(--header-h)+var(--toc-button-h))]" id="what-were-still-learning"><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 full-grid-content:@md:col-span-12 full-grid-content:@md:col-start-1 col-span-12 max-w-none"><h2 class="text-h3 scroll-mt-[calc(var(--header-h)+var(--toc-button-h))]"><span>What we’re still learning</span></h2></div></div></div><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 col-span-12 max-w-none [&amp;:not(:first-child)]:mt-sm"><p class="mb-sm last:mb-0"><span>This strategy has so far worked well up through internal launch and adoption at OpenAI. Building a real product for real users helped anchor our investments in reality and guide us towards long-term maintainability.</span></p></div><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 col-span-12 max-w-none [&amp;:not(:first-child)]:mt-sm"><p class="mb-sm last:mb-0"><span>What we don’t yet know is how architectural coherence evolves over years in a fully agent-generated system. We’re still learning where human judgment adds the most leverage and how to encode that judgment so it compounds. We also don’t know how this system will evolve as models continue to become more capable over time.</span></p></div><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 col-span-12 max-w-none [&amp;:not(:first-child)]:mt-sm"><p class="mb-sm last:mb-0"><span>What’s become clear: building software still demands discipline, but the discipline shows up more in the scaffolding rather than the code. The tooling, abstractions, and feedback loops that keep the codebase coherent are increasingly important.</span></p></div><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 col-span-12 max-w-none [&amp;:not(:first-child)]:mt-sm"><p class="mb-sm last:mb-0"><b><span>Our most difficult challenges now center on designing environments, feedback loops, and control systems</span></b><span> that help agents accomplish our goal: build and maintain complex, reliable software at scale.</span></p></div><div class="@md:col-span-6 @md:col-start-4 col-span-12 max-w-none [&amp;:not(:first-child)]:mt-sm"><p class="mb-sm last:mb-0"><span>As agents like Codex take on larger portions of the software lifecycle, these questions will matter even more. We hope that sharing some early lessons helps you reason about where to invest your effort so </span><a class="transition ease-curve-a duration-250 text-primary-100 hover:text-primary-60 relative
